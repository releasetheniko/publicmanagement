Introduction to Public Management

What is Public Management?

Public management is about managing public organizations — organizations characterized by the drive to create public value. It is done by public managers at different levels and is studied because the public sector is economically very large and its performance has direct societal consequences.

Exam building blocks
The exam tests three types of knowledge: Knowledge (concepts, theories), Insights (statements, articles), and Application (cases). All slides, classes, and articles are fair game.

Core vs. Dimensional Approach to "Public"

Core Approach (binary)

  • Legal type as the criterion: government-owned vs. privately-owned
  • Speaks to common sense — straightforward to apply
  • Limitation: misses hybrid organizations (universities, WADA, NPOs)
  • Case: WADA (World Anti-Doping Agency) — public mandate, private governance

Dimensional Approach (scale)

  • More or less public depending on externally imposed political authority
  • Risk: comparing apples and oranges when measuring
  • Three dimensions: % public funding (resources), agenda shift without public funding (goals), phone calls with government (communication)
  • Bozeman & Bretschneider (1994): both approaches are complementary

The Publicness Puzzle (Bozeman & Bretschneider, 1994)

Goal: test whether a dimensional understanding of publicness provides additional explanatory power over the core approach. Case: R&D labs — do public and private labs differ in their focus on patents/licenses (private output) vs. scientific publications (public output)?

Main finding: Both approaches are complementary. The broad-brush view ("does it matter?") needs to be combined with the fine-grained view ("if so, which dimensions matter?").
Three publicness dimensions: Resources (e.g., % grant funding), Goals/Agenda (e.g., "without government financing there would be a major shift in our R&D focus"), and Communication (e.g., frequency of contact with government personnel).
Practical implication: Organizations like universities have multiple legal forms, mixed funding (student fees + subsidies), and boards with politically appointed members — they score high on publicness dimensions even without being formally government-owned.

Who Are Public Managers?

Public managers operate at three hierarchical levels — top (head of organization), middle (head of division), and line (head of team/department). They are explicitly not street-level bureaucrats, who deliver services directly to citizens with frontline discretion.

Hammerschmid et al. (2016) — Survey of 6,701 Top EU Public Managers
25% have degrees in business/management/economics · 24% in law · 23% in engineering/natural sciences · 13% in political science · 15% in social sciences. And: 21% had at least 5 years in the private sector, 18% in the nonprofit sector. Public managers are not a single mold — they come from diverse disciplinary and sectoral backgrounds.

The Multi-Level Approach

Macro Level

  • Concepts and theories invariable for all PSOs
  • Reform paradigms (OPA, NPM, NPG)
  • Public value, performance frameworks
  • Red tape, governance systems

Meso Level

  • Organizational practices implemented differently per PSO
  • Strategic management, organizational scale
  • Mergers, networks, performance management
  • Context-dependent decisions

Micro Level

  • Relationships with key actors
  • Street-level bureaucrats (SLBs)
  • Politicians in the political-administrative sphere
  • Citizens as users, customers, co-producers
Inside vs. Outside
The multi-actor sphere outside the organization includes companies, nonprofits, consultancy firms, and universities. The societal sphere includes citizens. The political-administrative sphere includes politicians. Inside the organization are the different levels of management and SLBs. Public management is about navigating all of these simultaneously.

Practice & Exam Questions

Knowledge · 5 pts
What does it mean to say that publicness is "dimensional rather than binary"? Use the three dimensions of publicness to illustrate your answer with an example of a hybrid organization.
Rather than simply being "public" or "private," organizations exist on a continuum. Bozeman's three dimensions are: Resources (where does funding come from — taxes vs. market revenue?), Goals/Agenda (whose interests are served — the public broadly, or paying clients?), and Communication/Accountability (to whom must the organization answer?). A nonprofit hospital receives public subsidies (public resources), serves a broad community mission (public goals), but also competes for patients and charges fees (private elements) — making it a hybrid with partial publicness on each dimension.
Knowledge · 5 pts
Explain the difference between street-level bureaucrats and public managers. Why does this distinction matter for understanding public management?
Street-level bureaucrats (SLBs) are frontline workers (teachers, social workers, police officers) who interact directly with citizens and exercise discretion in applying policy. Public managers operate at meso or macro levels — they set strategy, allocate resources, and manage organizations rather than directly delivering services. The distinction matters because SLBs effectively make policy through their day-to-day decisions; managing them requires different tools (motivation, discretion management) than managing top-level administrators.
Essay · 10 pts
Public management is described as a multi-disciplinary and multi-level field. Discuss what this means in practice and why studying public management is relevant given the economic and societal role of the public sector.
Multi-disciplinary: public management draws on political science, sociology, economics, and organizational theory — no single discipline captures all of it. Multi-level: analysis spans micro (individual SLBs, discretion), meso (organizational management, performance), and macro (governance systems, reform paradigms). Relevance: the public sector is economically very large (major employer, major GDP share) and its performance directly shapes quality of life — health systems, education, social safety nets. Poor public management has real consequences. The positive relationship between management quality and public sector performance provides a direct empirical justification for the field.

Public Service Performance

The IOO Model — Conceptualizing Performance

Public sector performance is organized as an input–output–outcome chain. Inputs are resources fed in; outputs are directly measurable deliverables; outcomes are the broader societal effects. Performance is simultaneously multidimensional and multi-level.

Multidimensional: equity, efficiency, effectiveness — and more. Example from a leisure centre: acknowledging ethnic discrimination in sports (equity dimension) vs. introducing positive discrimination in team selection (program level) vs. a zero-tolerance culture against discrimination in every aspect of organizational life (organizational level).
Multi-level: organizational level (e.g., cost efficiency of the whole organization) vs. program level (e.g., inclusion within a specific service). These levels can produce contradictory findings.
Take-away #1
Public sector performance is an umbrella concept with multiple dimensions at different levels. There is no single "right" definition — what counts as performance is itself a political and strategic choice.

How to Measure? Technical Choices

Objective Measurement

  • Absence of social desirability bias (SDB) — the "golden standard"
  • Allows benchmarking across time and peer organizations
  • Often financial indicators; no third-party quality control
  • Risk: "not everything that counts can be counted"
  • Assumptions and political choices embedded in what is (not) included

Subjective Measurement

  • Allows cross-sector / cross-country comparison
  • Includes small organizations absent from secondary data
  • Convenient; perceptions matter in themselves
  • Risk: SDB and other cognitive biases
  • Mixed reliability when compared to objective measures

Additional design choices: single-item vs. multi-item scales. Multi-item scales reduce measurement error and capture more nuance but are more burdensome to collect. The evolution in nonprofit management research shows a clear trend toward multi-item, multi-source approaches.

Take-away #2 — From Technical to Political
What and how is measured is ultimately a function of individual belief systems — scientific, ideological, political. Example: a $2 trillion government spending figure can be framed as proof of wasteful bureaucracy (DOGE logic) or as essential service provision. Prof. Don Moynihan (Ford School) shows the "basic math" assumptions behind efficiency arguments often don't hold up empirically.

Why Measure? Two Rationales

Measure to Prove (Legitimacy)

  • Public accountability to external funders/government
  • Indicators typically defined externally
  • Tied to public funding — "looking backward"
  • Risk: "best image" mentality → necessary evil
  • Triggers: Oliver's (1991) strategic response strategies

Measure to Improve (Learning)

  • Internal organizational learning focus
  • Indicators defined internally — "looking forward"
  • Positive image: evaluation as inherent good management
  • "Vulnerable attitude" — accepting imperfection
  • Enables genuine discretion and improvement

Organizational Response Strategies (Oliver, 1991)

PSOs hold a multitude of strategic responses to external accountability pressures. The more the orientation is "measure to prove," the more creative — and sometimes deviant — these responses become.

Acquiescence: Full compliance with external requirements. Passive acceptance of indicators and standards.
Compromise: Partial compliance; balance competing internal and external demands. Negotiate indicator definitions.
Avoidance: Reduce the need to comply — buffer the organization from external scrutiny, conceal non-compliance.
Defiance: Active resistance, dismissal of norms or outright challenge of external pressures.
Manipulation: Attempt to actively change or control the content of external expectations themselves.
Key tension
How can accountability work purposefully — allowing sensible control AND organizational learning simultaneously? Purely rational management tool use may forget about organizational fit and the human factor.

Standardization vs. Discretion — The SSM Case (Matthys et al., 2024)

The self-sufficiency matrix (SSM) is a tool imposed by local government on social service PSOs (N=22). Intake and re-scoring every 3 months. Purpose: equality and intervention effectiveness.

Social Worker's Critique

  • "Very artificial — I couldn't care less about it"
  • Good days/bad days: rosy light bias
  • Neglects client's own perspective (dirty house ≠ low quality of life if client is fine with it)
  • Not all life domains equally scorable (finance vs. mental health)
  • Inadequate for complex cases — oversimplifies reality

Organizational Problems

  • Specialist PSOs don't cover all SSM domains (e.g., finance or housing may be out of scope)
  • High caseload → shortcuts to meet admin deadlines (copy & paste)
  • Ask colleagues → different scoring norms across organizations
  • Adoption (capacity/translation) ≠ Implementation (actual use)
  • Discretionary space: social workers navigate rather than comply

The Dark Side — Organizational Cheating (Bohte & Meier, 2000)

When performance is notoriously difficult to measure, it becomes prone to goal displacement. Case: Texas school districts under standardized TAAS testing. The theoretical logic: positive performance gap → pressure on administrators and students → non-legitimate exemptions from exams.

Cutting corners: "Beating the test" rather than the actual goal — preparing responsible citizens and enhancing economic self-sufficiency. Classic case of measuring the proxy instead of the thing itself.
Lying: Depicting more favorable results, or telling a selective truth to external audiences — emphasizing metrics that look good, burying those that don't.
Sampling bias (cherry-picking): Granting exam exemptions to low-income/minority students predicted to score poorly — inflating scores without improving underlying performance. The Bohte & Meier model shows administrator-to-student ratios and funding per student predict non-legitimate TAAS absences.
Take-away #3
Managing public sector performance is a situated practice and can have a "dark side." The more external accountability pressure, the more creative organizational responses — including deviant ones. This makes the design of accountability systems itself a critical managerial and political act.

Key Authors

Bohte & Meier (2000) Matthys et al. (2024) Oliver (1991) Don Moynihan

Practice & Exam Questions

Knowledge · 5 pts
Explain the IOO model of public sector performance. Why is performance considered "multidimensional" and why does this create challenges for public managers?
The IOO model (Input–Output–Outcome) traces how resources fed into an organization (inputs) are transformed into deliverables (outputs) that produce broader societal effects (outcomes). Performance is multidimensional because it encompasses equity, efficiency, effectiveness, and more — each measured at different levels (organizational vs. program). This creates challenges because: different stakeholders prioritize different dimensions; measuring multiple dimensions simultaneously requires trade-offs; and what counts as "good" performance is inherently political, not purely technical.
Knowledge · 5 pts
What is the difference between measuring to prove and measuring to improve? What organizational response strategies are associated with each rationale?
Measuring to prove (legitimacy): indicators are externally defined, tied to funding, and primarily serve public accountability — the organization looks backward and tries to present the best possible image. Measuring to improve (learning): indicators are internally defined, forward-looking, and used to drive better management decisions — a "vulnerable attitude" that accepts imperfection. Response strategies for "prove": gaming, cherry-picking, emphasizing favorable metrics. For "improve": genuine data use, discretionary adaptation (e.g., the self-sufficiency matrix).
Essay · 10 pts
Using the case of standardized testing in Texas (Bohte & Meier, 2000) and the self-sufficiency matrix (Matthys et al., 2024), discuss the conditions under which performance measurement leads to goal displacement. What are the managerial implications?
Goal displacement occurs when organizations optimize measured targets at the expense of the underlying mission. In Texas, school districts "beat the test" through cutting corners, lying, and sampling bias (excluding low performers). In the SSM case, social workers found the matrix too artificial, leading to shortcuts (copy-paste, ignoring client complexity). Conditions that enable displacement: performance is difficult to measure inherently; indicators are externally imposed with high stakes; discretion is constrained; organizational culture tolerates or incentivizes gaming. Managerial implications: involve frontline workers in indicator design; use multiple measures; audit for gaming behavior; build a culture of genuine improvement over image management.

Public Management Reforms

OPA, NPM, and NPG as normative prescriptions — lenses to study and templates to do public management.

  • Gemeinschaft → Gesellschaft transition
  • Rational/legal leadership (Weber)
  • Hierarchy, rules, neutral officials
  • Government should row
  • Citizens = voters · Theta values

NPM · Age of the Market

  • More management, measurement, market
  • Let managers manage · Competition
  • Government steers, not rows
  • Citizens = consumers · Sigma values
  • Hollow state → more rule layers

NPG · Age of Networks

  • From government to governance
  • Co-production and wicked issues
  • Government = convener, catalyst, collaborator
  • Citizens = co-producers · Landa values

What Are Public Management Reforms?

Definition
Public management reforms are normative prescriptions that offer direction on what is expected from public managers — which performance dimensions to prioritize and which values to prioritize. They are both lenses to study public management and templates to do it.
Key methodological point
These are "big concepts with different manifestations and intensities — and thus different effects." Context matters. The interaction between paradigms matters too: it is not a linear evolution but a layered reality. Real-world systems combine elements of all three.

OPA — The Age of Bureaucracy (Weber)

Weber's sociology traces the shift from small-N traditional communities (Gemeinschaft, charismatic/traditional leadership) to large-N modern societies (Gesellschaft, rational/legal leadership). The bureaucratic organization is the institutional response to this modernization.

Hierarchical organization: Top management decides, line managers execute. Top-down control and discipline throughout.
Clear lines of authority + rule-driven: Standardization of public service delivery to guarantee equality, efficiency, and predictability. Equal treatment is both the goal and the method.
Expert bureaucrats: Formal education, well-defined functions. Knowledge as the basis of authority.
Neutral officials: Neutrality toward (1) service users and (2) politicians — the classic decision/execution divide.
Merit-based advancement: Years of experience and performance level; permanent positions protect from arbitrary dismissal.

Public management from this perspective: government needs to "row" — designing and implementing policy. Citizens = voters. Core values: theta values — keep it honest and fair.

Criticisms of OPA

Interpersonal Level

  • Excessive rule compliance — losing sight of the spirit
  • Street-level discretion emerges despite rules (SSM case)
  • Clear authority + rule-driven ≠ actual behavior

Organizational Level

  • Orgs advance their own goals (growth) over public value
  • J. Edgar Hoover: 48 years directing FBI under 8 presidents
  • Used FBI to harass dissidents, amass personal power — "neutral official" as fiction

Societal Level

  • Administrative burden — rules designed for non-take-up
  • US abortion policy: 1/3 of restrictions create burden by design (Herd & Moynihan)
  • Learning, compliance & psychological costs → equality undermined

NPM — The Age of the Market

NPM frames Weberian bureaucracy as a failed model: government too big, too unsustainable, too bureaucratic. Prescription: more management, measurement and market — government should work like a business.

NPM Building Blocks

  • Let managers manage — room for operational decisions
  • Business tools: strategic planning, lean management
  • Do more with less — economy, efficiency, effectiveness (3Es)
  • Cut large orgs into smaller single-objective units (~manageability)
  • Competition + contracting + control = quality mechanism
  • Government steers, not rows · Citizens = consumers · Sigma values

Pollitt & Bouckaert: Cross-Country Variation

  • Anglo-Saxon world furthest ahead (UK, NZ, Australia)
  • Belgium in the middle of the pack
  • Spectrum: Maintain → Modernize → Marketize → Minimize
  • Different intensities produce different outcomes
  • Context matters — no one-size-fits-all NPM

NPM in Practice — Inside and Outside Public Organizations

NPM is everywhere inside public organizations (UK £2.5bn consulting market in 2021; $25bn globally in 2023) — healthcare, environmental affairs, crisis management, defence, IT, policy development.

Consultants — Arguments For

  • Substantive experts delivering fast results
  • Infuse expert knowledge into slow bureaucracies
  • Offer innovative approaches to difficult issues (civic participation, municipal amalgamations)
  • Stamp of approval — legitimizing public policy

Consultants — Arguments Against

  • Cookie-cutter solutions to context-sensitive issues
  • Political illiteracy — miss public sector complexity
  • Leave with information; don't share, use for future assignments elsewhere
  • Crowding out civil servants (permanence of "temporary" services)
  • Politicians: blame avoidance by shifting responsibility
The Hollow State — Privatization paradox
NPM created the "hollow state": national government leaks responsibilities up (to supra-national bodies), out (to civil society and companies), and down (to local governments). Privatization = short-term gain (deflected responsibility, public income) vs. long-term costs for citizens and poor quality. The promise of "outsourcing" public services to competition has frequently broken down — Britain was a pioneer in outsourcing; now, "the model is broken."

NPG — The Age of Networks

NPG starts from the observation that government is fragmented — many public organizations, levels, and actors. From government to governance: other societal actors have a responsibility to steer society. Focus on wicked issues (e.g., Covid-19, global warming, poverty, inequality).

Government's role: Convener, catalyst, collaborator — sometimes steering, sometimes rowing, sometimes partnering, sometimes staying out of the way. Very different from OPA (always rows) and NPM (always steers).
Citizens = co-producers: Active involvement in policy formulation (e.g., input in mobility plans) and policy implementation (e.g., co-opening a community swimming place). Core values: Landa values — keep it robust and resilient.
The public manager as boundary spanner: Given increased interdependencies between state and non-state actors, collaboration across different service fields (e.g., poverty), and civic participation — public managers must engage in coordination and collaboration across organizational and service field boundaries to deliver more integrated services.
A layered reality — the PISA case
The PISA (education assessment) case illustrates how real systems combine all three paradigms: hierarchical standardized testing (OPA), competition and performance league tables (NPM), and international collaboration and network-based policy learning (NPG). No single paradigm dominates — they coexist and interact. This is why context-sensitive "middle-range theory" is more useful than "grand theory" for practitioners.

Key Authors

Weber Pollitt & Bouckaert Osborne (2006) Herd & Moynihan (2018) George et al. (2020)

Practice & Exam Questions

Knowledge · 5 pts
List and explain the five building blocks of Weberian bureaucracy (OPA). For each, identify one criticism raised in the lecture.
1. Hierarchical organization — top-down control; criticism: creates rigidity and slow adaptation. 2. Clear lines of authority — standardized delivery; criticism: equality of process ≠ equity of outcome. 3. Expert bureaucrats — merit and specialization; criticism: expertise becomes siloed, limits cross-cutting coordination. 4. Neutral officials — separates politics from administration; criticism: J. Knowledge · 5 pts
How does NPM view the role of government and the citizen differently from OPA? What core values underpin each paradigm?
OPA: government should "row" — design and directly implement policy. Citizens are voters providing a democratic mandate. Core values: theta values (honesty, fairness, equality through standardization). NPM: government should "steer, not row" — decide which services are core tasks and outsource the rest. Citizens are consumers with individual choices. Core values: sigma values (economy, efficiency, effectiveness). The key shift is from rule-following equality to market-based efficiency, and from citizens as collective mandate-givers to individual customers.
Essay · 10 pts
Compare and contrast OPA, NPM, and NPG as approaches to public management reform. For each paradigm, discuss: (1) the core value orientation, (2) the role of the citizen, and (3) one key empirical criticism or limitation.
OPA — values: theta (fairness, equality); citizen as voter; criticism: administrative burden, rigidity, risk of bureaucratic capture (Hoover). NPM — values: sigma (economy, efficiency, effectiveness); citizen as consumer; criticism: fragmentation through agencies, hollow state, more rules despite claiming to cut bureaucracy, information asymmetry undermines real consumer choice. NPG — values: phi (openness, participation, trust); citizen as co-producer; criticism: governance networks can lack accountability, co-production risk of government abdicating responsibility, impact depends heavily on administrative tradition (Voorberg et al.). Each paradigm emerged as a critique of the previous — but none fully replaces the others; real systems are mixes (Pollitt & Bouckaert).

Public Value(s)

Why Public Values? — Societal Context

Public values are not static — they shift as society changes. Six key societal trends currently pressure the public sector: climate change and adaptation, urbanization, growing aging population and rising healthcare costs, resource scarcity, (inter)national security pressures, and increasing poverty and inequality.

The Democratic Triangle
No single entity — government, market, or civil society — possesses all the necessary resources and insights on its own. Collaboration and co-creation within the democratic triangle are essential. This is why public value management is inherently relational and context-dependent.

Understanding Public Value(s) — Three Academic Lenses

Political Science

  • Politics = authoritative allocation of values (Easton, 1965)
  • Values solidified into binding policy
  • Four core policy questions (Hemerijck): Does it work? Does it fit? Is it allowed? Is it appropriate?
  • Division of political portfolios shows value priorities

Public Administration

  • TPA: government as legal authority — constitutional legality
  • NPM: government as service provider — instrumental efficiency
  • Post-NPM: government as arena for value creation — all four questions, especially feasibility and acceptability
  • Shift from hierarchy → market → network

Public Management

  • Moore (1995): managers create value through strategic activities
  • Strategy is not something an org "has" but actively "does"
  • Strategy-as-Practice: actors, institutions, activities
  • Internal and external stakeholder management

Two Competing Definitions

Values Assessment Perspective (Bozeman, 2007)

  • Public values = normative consensus about the principles on which governments and policies should be based
  • Focus: which values qualify as "public"? Value pluralism, value conflicts, value trade-offs
  • Values are "solidified" into normative consensus through political processes
  • Problem: consensus is contested and evolving

Value Creation Perspective (Moore, 1995)

  • Public value = all benefits that government activity delivers for society
  • Focus: strategic activities of public managers to create value
  • Public value proposition depends on context — not universal
  • Operationalized through Moore's Strategic Triangle

Moore's Strategic Triangle (1995)

To create public value, a public manager must simultaneously align three elements — neglecting any one of them undermines the others.

Public value proposition: What value is the organization trying to create? What problem is it solving for whom? This must be articulated clearly and updated as societal needs change.
Legitimacy (Authorizing environment): Policymakers, resource holders, oversight bodies — those with coercive power who authorize the mission and provide resources. Public managers must maintain political and social legitimacy.
Operational capacity: The organization's ability to actually deliver on its value proposition — staff, systems, processes, capabilities. Without this, even well-authorized missions fail in practice.
Why this connects to political astuteness (L08)
The strategic triangle explains why public managers need political astuteness: aligning the authorizing environment requires actively managing relationships with politicians and stakeholders, not just technically delivering services. This is a recurrent theme throughout the course.

Key Distinction: Values vs. Value

Public Values (plural)

  • What society expects from the public sector
  • Normative consensus — collective but individually interpreted
  • "In the eye of the beholder"
  • Difficult to translate into organizational action
  • Examples: equity, accountability, efficiency, sustainability, democracy

Public Value (singular)

  • The extent to which public values are actually met
  • The end goal of the public manager
  • Created in a task environment (sector-specific)
  • Requires aligning Moore's three corners
  • Can be created and destroyed simultaneously

Value Conflicts in Hybrid Organizations (Beaton et al., 2021)

The Promenade Conservancy case illustrates how value conflicts emerge when organizations pursue multiple public values simultaneously. The director (MBA graduate) introduced food trucks to make the promenade "vibrant" and financially stable.

Vibrancy vs. Aesthetics/Security/Morale: Food trucks created vibrancy but ruined the iconic view (trucks parked in front), created safety issues (vehicles in a pedestrian zone), produced exhaust and trash, and demoralised park rangers who felt ignored.
Commons vs. Stability: Nonprofits set up awareness booths (distracting food truck clients); free cupcake samples displaced truck business; homeless people sleeping on benches discouraged diners.
Key insight: Public management may be imperfect by design. Bounded rationality means managers focus on the most salient values and may unintentionally destroy less visible ones. Creation and destruction happen simultaneously.

Managing Public Values in Practice

Central Argument
Managing public values is a situated practice. Public managers must distil normative consensus from stakeholders and translate it into organizational action — navigating value prioritization, conflicts, and the risk of value destruction. The real estate case (Huijbregts et al.) illustrates this: decisions about public buildings require balancing financial sustainability, cultural heritage, accessibility, environmental standards, and democratic accountability simultaneously.

Practice & Exam Questions

Knowledge · 5 pts
Explain the distinction between "public values" and "public value." Why is this distinction important for public managers?
Public values (plural) = the normative standards society expects public organizations to uphold — equity, accountability, sustainability, efficiency, etc. They are collective but also individually interpreted ("in the eye of the beholder"). Public value (singular) = the extent to which those values are actually met — the end goal of the public manager, tied to Moore's strategic triangle (authorizing environment, task environment, operational capabilities). The distinction matters because managers must first understand what values are expected (plural) before they can pursue the outcome (singular) — and navigating conflicts between competing values is itself the central managerial challenge.
Knowledge · 5 pts
Using the Promenade Conservancy case (Beaton et al., 2021), explain how public value creation and public value destruction can occur simultaneously in a hybrid organization.
The Promenade Conservancy director pursued vibrancy and financial stability through food trucks — creating value on those dimensions. But simultaneously, food trucks destroyed value on other dimensions: aesthetics (blocking the iconic view), security (trucks in a pedestrian zone), environmental quality (exhaust, trash), and staff morale (rangers felt ignored). Because the organization managed multiple public values at once (a hybrid), optimizing one value necessarily created tensions with others. This illustrates that public management may be imperfect by design — bounded rationality means managers focus on the most salient values and may unintentionally destroy less visible ones.
Essay · 10 pts
Critically assess the concept of public value. Why has it been called "a fuzzy idea rather than a stable umbrella concept" (Cui & Osborne, 2021)? What are the practical implications of value conflicts for public managers?
"Fuzzy" because: public value has been defined inconsistently across the literature; it conflates process (how decisions are made) with outcome (what is achieved); and it is difficult to operationalize — distilling normative consensus from diverse stakeholders is a deeply political act. Practical implications of value conflicts: managers must engage in situated value prioritization — there is no universal ranking of values, so context and stakeholder input determine what takes precedence. Conflicts cannot be fully resolved, only navigated. Managers need frameworks for surfacing hidden trade-offs, involving affected parties, and making transparent choices. The risk of inaction is value destruction by default.

Big vs. Small — The Evidence (Walker et al., 2023)

Big is Better (Economic Perspective)

  • Economies of scale → reduced overhead and supply costs
  • Improved bargaining position with suppliers
  • Broader range of integrated activities (something for everyone)
  • Better HR: competitive advantage to recruit and retain staff

Small is Beautiful (Political Perspective)

  • Standardized services — lack tailored approach
  • Less connection with service recipients → reduced responsiveness
  • High coordination costs
  • Distant from frontline needs and local knowledge
Verdict
No evidence supports either the "big is better" or "small is beautiful" camp unconditionally. The relationship may be curvilinear ("too much of a good thing"). Size is something to manage for — not a deterministic variable.

Merger Case: Nonprofit-Public Elderly Care (Vanhoutte, 2023)

A 2014 merger between a public and nonprofit elderly care centre illustrates the organizational and human complexity of mergers in practice.

A merger does not mean integration. Staff remained segregated (symbolized by chairs moved apart at the first joint New Year's reception). Shared physical space ≠ shared organizational culture.
Organizational benefits: Leadership stability, financial stability, stronger position with banks for building loans, improved connections with local government.
Individual costs: Stress from job security concerns, different salaries for the same work (phasing out), different procedures and routines disrupting established practices.

Self-Determination Theory and Change Acceptance

Research on a nonprofit serving vulnerable children (N=129) found that models explained over 60% of variation in support for organizational change.

Relatedness (meaningful participation): Staff need to feel meaningfully involved — not just consulted.
Autonomy: Asking upward and sideways, not top-down imposition. "Not only giving voice, but also feeling heard."
Competence: Is the change purposeful for me? Staff need to understand how the change is relevant to their own work.

Municipal Mergers and Administrative Capacity

Beyond nonprofit-public mergers, municipal mergers are a key trend in Belgian and European public management. Growing importance of strong local administrations because of the "leaking down" (hollow state) of responsibilities — childcare, spatial planning, libraries, youth work, policing.

Administrative capacity signals: One-person services (spatial planning, IT, mobility, sustainability) and no-person services (e.g., no administrative support for representation in intercommunal networks) indicate local governments at breaking point.
Why it matters: In-house expertise is critical to decrease dependence on external actors — consultants, real estate developers — who may not serve public interests. The "hollow state" requires local governments to either merge or build networks to compensate for individual capacity gaps.
Networks as alternative to mergers: Wicked issues (cross-cutting, intractable problems) require multi-actor governance networks. Networks allow coordination without structural merger — but introduce their own challenges of accountability, coordination costs, and rule accumulation (→ red tape, L06).

Practice & Exam Questions

Knowledge · 5 pts
What does the meta-analysis by Walker et al. (2023) conclude about the relationship between organizational size and public service performance? Why is this result important for management practice?
Walker et al. find no linear relationship — neither "big is better" nor "small is beautiful" holds unconditionally. The relationship may be curvilinear ("too much of a good thing"). This is important because it reframes the question: rather than asking "should we be bigger or smaller?", managers should ask "how do we manage for size?" Mergers and splits are not inherently good or bad — their outcomes depend on how they are managed. The economic arguments for scale (efficiency, bargaining power) and the political arguments for smallness (responsiveness, tailoring) can both be valid simultaneously, requiring managers to actively navigate the trade-offs.
Essay · 10 pts
Using the elderly care merger case (Vanhoutte, 2023) and self-determination theory, explain why public sector mergers often fail to achieve integration. What do managers need to consider beyond the organizational and financial rationale?
The elderly care case shows that a merger on paper (legal, financial, structural) does not produce integration in practice — staff literally moved their chairs apart at the first joint event. Mergers fail at the human level because they ignore self-determination theory needs: Relatedness (staff need meaningful participation, not just notification), Autonomy (bottom-up involvement, feeling heard, not just giving voice), and Competence (the change must feel personally relevant and purposeful). Beyond financial logic, managers need to: communicate why the change matters to individuals; involve staff in how it is implemented; create structures for genuine voice (all-staff meetings, workgroups); and recognize that different organizational cultures don't merge automatically — they need active bridging over time.

Red Tape

Defining, originating, and combating the rules that burden without benefit.

Bozeman 2000 George et al. 2020 Meta-analysis Digitalization

Mind Map

Definition
Rules with high compliance burden + low functionality (Bozeman, 2000) · "One person's red tape is another's treasured safeguard"
Types
Internal (born bad / gone bad) vs. External (government to third parties) — different drivers, different leverage
Origins
OPA (equality through standardization) · NPM (more accountability = more rules) · NPG (networks add rule layers)
Effects
Negative impact on both organizational performance and employee outcomes (George et al., 2021) — small-to-medium effect

Digitalization · Red tape reduction programs · Simplify, support, reach out

Defining Red Tape (Bozeman, 2000)

Foundational Definition
Rules, regulations and procedures that remain in force and entail a compliance burden but do not advance the legitimate purposes the rules were intended to serve.

Red tape requires rules to score high on two characteristics simultaneously:

Compliance burden: Effort and time required for designated stakeholders to comply with the rule.
Lack of functionality: The extent to which the rule fails to serve the purpose it was intended to regulate.
Key insight: "One person's red tape is another's treasured safeguard." Assessing red tape is inherently subjective — it involves judging whether the trouble of complying outweighs the perceived benefits.

Internal vs. External Red Tape

Internal Red Tape

  • Rules born bad: wrong starting assumptions
  • Rules gone bad: wrong implementation or unresponsive over time
  • Directly influenced by organizational leadership
  • Can be changed more readily by managers

External Red Tape

  • Government to third parties — regulations & accountability
  • High when many different stakeholders involved
  • High when discretion is low
  • Harder to change (~requires lobbying)

Origins of Red Tape

OPA: All citizens should be treated equally — many standardized rules implemented, creating a compliance baseline.
NPM (1980s): Increased focus on performance management, external accountability, and contracting → more rules imposed by government. Hollow state → more governmental layers → more rule-making.
NPG (2000s): From government to governance → networks and co-production → additional rule layers from multiple actors.

Effects Meta-Analysis (George et al., 2021)

Key Finding
Red tape has a significant, negative, and small-to-medium impact on both organizational performance (E&E) and employee outcomes (job satisfaction, motivation). NOT as apocalyptic as often assumed — but continuous attention is required because it tends to re-accumulate.

Main Effect Mechanisms

  • Hampers recruitment, retention & motivation of qualified personnel
  • Hard to obtain useful performance information in a timely manner
  • Lack of discretion hurts motivation → lower job satisfaction and stress
  • Effect is real but small-to-medium — not catastrophic

Context & Method Moderators

  • Internal red tape is more harmful than external (within direct manager control)
  • Limited variation across sectors and administrative traditions — careful to generalize
  • No CMB bias: multi-source data is more reliable
  • Other contextual variables: organizational size, task, age, time period

In Search of Silver Bullets — Digitalization

Arguments For Digitalization

  • Centralization of information → decrease in compliance costs
  • Increase in functionality (perceived as more efficient and responsive)
  • Service finders, eligibility calculators, one-stop counters
  • Flemish renovations portal: interactive eligibility display

Risks and Limitations

  • Existing bureaucratic processes merely replaced by digital alternatives
  • Digital tools can shift red tape (e.g., from central to local level)
  • Convenience of digital tools → more accountability demands → more rules
  • Regulating AI/digital tools through new rules creates new red tape

Remedies — How to Deal with Red Tape

The "lasagna" metaphor: accumulated rule layers (OPA + NPM + NPG) that are difficult to separate. Reducing red tape requires both diagnosis and deliberate action.

Policymaker's perspective (external): WHAT — list to find overlap and non-functional aspects, then cut/streamline. HOW — verify compliance processes and streamline them. The process matters: give stakeholders voice and the feeling of being heard.
Public manager's perspective (internal): (1) Cut by giving voice — involve frontline staff in identifying red tape. (2) Allow discretion to SLBs. (3) Explain and justify rules — even if they can't be removed, transparent rationale reduces perceived burden.
SLB's perspective: Be transparent about (1) what is perceived as red tape and why, and (2) how it is navigated in practice. "Death by a thousand 10-minute tasks" — small compliance requirements accumulate into major burden (UGent VPN as a daily example).
Size and red tape
Larger organizations are better equipped to deal with red tape (dedicated compliance staff, legal expertise, economies of scale in processing) — but also more likely to generate internal red tape themselves through formalization. Size is both a buffer and a source.

Key Authors

Bozeman (2000) George et al. (2021) Matthys et al. (2024)

Practice & Exam Questions

Knowledge · 5 pts
Define red tape using Bozeman's (2000) foundational definition. Explain the two key characteristics a rule must have to qualify as red tape, and give one example of each measurement approach (objective and subjective).
Bozeman: red tape = rules, regulations and procedures that remain in force and entail a compliance burden but do not advance the legitimate purposes the rules were intended to serve. Two characteristics: (1) Compliance burden — high effort/time required to comply; (2) Lack of functionality — the rule fails to serve its intended purpose. Objective measurement: count the number of steps, forms, or days required to complete a specified process (e.g., verifying how many forms are needed to register a new business). Subjective measurement: survey employees — e.g., "The rules and procedures in my organization make it difficult to get my work done" (Likert scale).
Knowledge · 5 pts
All organizations can experience internal red tape, but governments are typically the main source of external red tape. Explain this statement using OPA, NPM, and NPG as drivers of red tape.
Internal red tape affects any organization (rules born bad or gone bad). External red tape flows from government to third parties. OPA generated red tape through its emphasis on equal treatment — standardized rules for every situation created compliance burden without proportionate benefit. NPM paradoxically increased red tape while claiming to reduce it: more performance accountability, more contracting rules, more reporting requirements, and the hollow state created multiple governmental layers each adding rules. NPG added further layers through governance networks — co-production and multi-actor arrangements require rules to coordinate actors, adding to the cumulative burden.
Essay · 10 pts
Based on the meta-analysis by George et al. (2021), assess the empirical evidence on the effects of red tape on organizational performance and employee outcomes. Under what conditions are these effects strongest, and what are the managerial implications?
George et al. find a significant, negative, small-to-medium effect of red tape on both organizational performance (efficiency, effectiveness) and employee outcomes (job satisfaction, motivation). Mechanisms: red tape hampers recruitment/retention of qualified staff; prevents managers from accessing timely performance information; and reduces employee discretion, lowering motivation. Conditions where effects are strongest: internal red tape (directly influenced by leadership, so more salient); single-source data (CMB inflates effect sizes, so multi-source findings are more reliable); public sector (vs. private). Managerial implications: reducing red tape is worthwhile but requires sustained attention (it tends to re-accumulate); internal red tape offers more leverage than external; distinguish between rules that burden without purpose vs. rules that serve a genuine safeguarding function.

Strategic Management & Planning

Prof. dr. Kenn Meyfroodt · Theoretical grounding, resource-based view, behavioral strategy, and public sector context.

Mission · Vision · Goals Resource-Based View Bryson et al. 2007 Nutt & Backoff 1993 Behavioral Strategy

Vocabulary of Strategy (Johnson et al., 2008)

Mission
Overriding purpose in line with values or expectations of stakeholders
Vision
Desired future state — the aspiration of the organization
Goal
General statement of aim or purpose
Objective
Quantification or precise statement of the goal
Strategy
Long-term direction · Aligning aspirations and capabilities
Control
Monitoring action steps — assess effectiveness, modify as necessary

What is Strategic Management? (Poister & Streib, 1999)

Conceptualizing Strategy (George, 2021)
Strategy answers three questions: WHY (public value creation), WHAT (goal achievement), and HOW (aligning aspirations and capabilities). It is a holistic process, not a single planning exercise. Strategy is not something a public organization "has" but something it actively "does."

Strategic management is characterized by "Babylonian confusion of tongues" — the terms mission, vision, strategy, plan are used inconsistently across both practice and research. The vocabulary above (Johnson et al.) provides a shared framework.

Approaches to Strategic Management (Nutt & Backoff, 1993)

Analytical Approach (Problem focus)

  • Portfolio approaches (BCG matrix etc.)
  • Industry analysis (Porter's five forces)
  • Rational planning systems
  • Systematic, stepwise, chronological
  • Formalised design approach

Actor & Agency Focus

  • Stakeholder analysis and management
  • Interpretative and adaptive strategy
  • Issue management
  • Strategic leadership (leader's personality and reputation central)
  • Politicized strategic management: negotiation & power

Four Strategic Logics (Idenburg, 1992)

High Goal / Low Process → Rational planning

  • Clear objectives, formal sequential steps
  • Risk: inflexible in turbulent environments

High Goal / High Process → Logical incrementalism

  • "You learn as you go" — purposeful but adaptive
  • Clear vision updated by experience and learning

Low Goal / High Process → Learning organization

  • Continuous innovation based on knowledge and competences
  • Vision-oriented culture; emergent not planned

Low Goal / Low Process → Ad hoc / Imposed

  • Strategy externally imposed at a higher level
  • Can be both a curse (loss of autonomy) and a blessing (clarity)
  • In reality: combinations of logics are most common

The Resource-Based View (Bryson et al., 2007)

The RBV is an "inside-out" approach: competitive advantage comes from the resources and capabilities an organization already has, not just from analyzing the external environment (Porter's "outside-in" five forces approach).

In the Public Sector
Public organizations must identify and build strategic capabilities that enable them to produce as much public value as possible at a responsible cost. Neglecting these capabilities results in struggling to achieve objectives, creating little public value, responding imperfectly to environmental changes, and facing legitimacy challenges.
Resources: Any assets the organization might draw on to achieve its goals — e.g., strengths in a SWOT analysis.
Competencies: A subset of resources — abilities, sets of actions, technologies, or processes that help perform well.
Distinctive competencies: Very difficult for others to replicate — uniqueness, rarity, inimitability, value. Source of enduring advantage.
Core distinctive competency: A distinctive competency whose presence is crucial for goal achievement — remove it, and goal achievement is unlikely.

The Livelihood Scheme (Bryson et al., 2007)

A practical tool that maps the relationship between critical success factors, resources, and competencies — linking what must be done (CSFs) to what the organization is capable of doing.

Process (7 steps): Preparation → Tentative mission and goal system → Identify CSFs → Identify distinctive competencies → Create full livelihood scheme → Develop strategic plan → Develop annual business plan.
Critical Success Factors: Things the organization must do, criteria it must meet, KPIs it must perform against. The external demand side.
Value–rarity–organizational embeddedness–imperfect imitability: The four characteristics that make a resource strategically valuable (Barney, 2001).

Strategic Management in the Public Sector Context

Political Component

  • Guiding principles often vaguer than in private sector
  • Political timeframe: short-term over long-term
  • Political majority coalitions: stability matters
  • Politics-administration relationship shapes strategy space
  • Political ambiguity is structural, not accidental

Decision-Making Processes

  • Transparency demands → decisions become public
  • Stakeholder pressure to influence strategy
  • Consensus decision-making and compromising
  • Decisions "reopened" as impact becomes clear in implementation
  • Publicness of debate ↔ strategic flexibility

Public Management Component

  • Multitude of goals — often conflicting
  • Performance measurement is difficult
  • Intangible products and services
  • Diversity of target groups and stakeholders
  • Lack of experience with strategic management

Behavioral Strategy (Powell et al., 2011)

Definition
Behavioral strategy merges cognitive and social psychology with strategic management theory and practice. It aims to bring realistic assumptions about human cognition, emotions, and social behavior to the strategic management of organizations — enriching strategy theory, empirical research, and real-world practice.

Key concept: managerial cognition — the cognitive processes by which managers acquire, interpret, and act upon information. Strategic planning decisions are not purely rational; they are shaped by biases, social dynamics, and information processing limitations. This connects to the behavioral turn in Public Administration (Grimmelikhuijsen et al., 2017).

Practice & Exam Questions

Knowledge · 5 pts
Explain the vocabulary of strategic management (mission, vision, goal, objective, strategy, control) using a public sector example of your choice.
Using a public hospital as an example: Mission = "provide accessible, high-quality healthcare to all residents regardless of ability to pay." Vision = "be the leading regional hospital for patient outcomes by 2030." Goal = "reduce waiting times in emergency care." Objective = "reduce average ER waiting time from 4 hours to 90 minutes by December 2026." Strategy = invest in triage redesign and add nurse practitioners. Essay · 10 pts
Using the approaches identified by Nutt & Backoff (1993) and Idenburg, discuss how strategic management in public organizations differs from the private sector. Why is a purely analytical approach to strategy insufficient in the public sector context?
Nutt & Backoff identify three types: analytical (portfolio, industry analysis, rational planning), actor-focused (stakeholder management, interpretive strategy), and agency-focused (strategic leadership, politicized management). Idenburg adds a process dimension: rational planning (high goal / low process) vs. logical incrementalism (high goal / high process) vs. learning organization (low goal / high process). In the public sector, purely analytical approaches are insufficient because: political mandates shift with election cycles; multiple and conflicting stakeholders (not just shareholders) must be satisfied; democratic accountability constrains strategic choices that would be routine in business; and wicked issues (cross-cutting, intractable) resist analytical decomposition. Public strategy is inherently political — it requires astuteness, coalition-building, and legitimacy management alongside analytical planning. In reality, combinations of approaches are used.

Managing Politicians

Mind Map

Old View

Political Astuteness
Active sense-making · Securing mandates · Enlisting operational capacity · Boundary-spanning

The Great Divide — Old View

Politics (Normative)

  • Passionate & normative — policymaking
  • Politicians: may prolong their power (self-interest)
  • Rogue principals — renege on authority constraints
  • Rotate in, lack expert knowledge

Administration (Rational)

  • Rational, neutral, equal — implementing policy
  • Public managers: may advance their career (self-interest)
  • Agents gone bad — use discretion against principal's goals
  • Expert knowledge but risk of capture
The Double Principal-Agent Problem
Central assumption: actors behave out of self-interest. Politicians and public managers both face incentives to deviate from the public interest. Information asymmetry (expert knowledge vs. rotational knowledge; state vs. street) makes monitoring difficult — requiring both internal and external control mechanisms.

Bureaucracy's Response — Protecting Against Rogue Principals

Expert bureaucrats through specialized training: Expertise gives public managers authority to push back — not just on technical grounds but as a professional obligation.
Integrity norms: Instill a set of norms that regulate the political-administrative relationship. Knowing when to be unresponsive is as important as knowing when to respond.
Merit-based advancement: Protects public managers from political retaliation when they choose normative constraints over responsiveness.

Responsiveness vs. Normative Constraints — Dilemmas (Christensen & Opstrup, 2017)

The tension between responsiveness (doing what politicians want) and normative constraints (upholding professional and legal standards) plays out differently depending on context. Constraining norms are not clearly written down — different people adhere to different norms.

Country / Admin. Tradition

  • Different expectations of neutrality
  • Scandinavian vs. Mediterranean traditions
  • Westminster vs. continental models

Organizational Characteristics

  • Resource munificence vs. scarcity
  • Advisory role (ministry/cabinet) vs. implementing role (agency/department)
  • Proximity to political principal matters

Individual Characteristics

  • Educational background
  • Higher hierarchical position → more exposure to political pressure
  • Generalist vs. specialist orientation
Real-world example — Jim Mattis & Trump (Woodward, 2018)
Bob Woodward's book reports that Defense Secretary Mattis compared Trump to a "fifth or sixth grader" in terms of strategic understanding. Mattis repeatedly resisted or delayed presidential orders he viewed as professionally or legally untenable — a textbook example of the responsiveness vs. normative constraints dilemma played out at the highest level. Eventually resigned when the tension became irreconcilable. This illustrates that the dilemma affects even the most senior political appointees.

Beyond the Divide — Modern View

Government has grown strongly — difficult for politicians to maintain overview. Training rate of civil servants has greatly increased; expertise gap has widened.
Contractualization and agencies — remote tasks require managers to exercise judgment in ambiguous authority structures with limited day-to-day political oversight.
Wicked issues — cross-cutting, intractable problems (poverty, climate, migration) cannot be solved instrumentally; they require expert judgment, political navigation, and network coordination simultaneously.
Not a line but a zone: The politics-administration boundary is dynamic — it changes according to situation, history, issues, and the personalities involved. More often ally than opponent.

Political Astuteness (Hartley et al., 2014)

Mixed-methods research: survey + interviews with senior and middle public managers in Australia, UK & New Zealand. Questions: to what extent are public managers "political"? How legitimate is this?

Active sense-making: Public managers test the waters — "is this what you want to happen?" They disentangle the bigger picture and have an intelligent understanding of politicians' worldview and their political reality. The public manager as sparring partner safeguarding public value.
Securing a mandate: Mandates are negotiated, adapted, and sometimes bartered — not simply received. Managers act as proxy for politicians in relationship-building with stakeholders. They explain policy so stakeholders may disagree but understand the thinking behind it.
Enlisting operational capacity: Understand power (im)balances, factions, and subgroups within the organization and its environment. Build coalitions. The public manager as boundary spanner.
Legitimacy question
Public managers are appointed, not democratically elected — so their "political" activity raises accountability questions. But evidence shows political activities are typically constructive, in support of policymakers. A further complication: politicians sometimes do not know exactly what they want, calling for expert knowledge as well as political judgment. The relationship is more often ally than opponent.

Guerrilla Government — From Collaboration to Conflict

Definition
Public servants disobeying the wishes of their superiors — either directly or indirectly communicated — to do what they perceive is the "right thing." Weaponizing discretion for ethical ends. The dark side of public management.
Wide array of strategies — from passive to active: Delaying, slow-rolling, selective implementation, leaking to the press, whistleblowing, formal complaints, resignation. Not all guerrilla activity is created equal — some is ethically justified; some undermines democratic accountability.
The ethical question: What is "right" when professional norms conflict with political instructions? The guerrilla government literature forces a confrontation with the limits of neutrality and the obligations of professional expertise.
Last resort principle: A good pitch goes a long way — making the professional case effectively through legitimate channels is always the first response. Guerrilla activity is justified only when legitimate channels are exhausted and fundamental norms are at stake.

Practice & Exam Questions

Knowledge · 5 pts
Explain the "double principal-agent problem" in the politics-administration relationship. What mechanisms does bureaucracy use to address this problem?
The double principal-agent problem: both politicians (principals) and public managers (agents) have self-interested incentives that may diverge from the public interest. Politicians may "go rogue" — overstepping legal authority or making politically motivated decisions. Public managers may "go bad" — using their information advantage and discretion to advance their career rather than the principal's goals. Information asymmetry (expert knowledge vs. rotational knowledge; state vs. street) makes monitoring difficult in both directions. Essay · 10 pts
Using Hartley et al. (2014), evaluate the concept of political astuteness. To what extent is it legitimate for public managers to be "political" in their work? Use the public value framework to structure your argument.
Hartley et al. find that public managers are actively political through: sense-making and testing mandates with politicians; negotiating/adapting mandates with stakeholders; building coalitions to enlist operational capacity. This is mostly constructive, not adversarial. Legitimacy argument via public value framework: to create public value, managers must align the authorizing environment (politicians, resource holders), the task environment, and operational capabilities — this requires political astuteness by definition. If managers are purely neutral implementers, they cannot effectively navigate the authorizing environment. Who Are Street-Level Bureaucrats?

SLBs are frontline public service workers who interact directly with citizens and exercise discretion in how they apply policy. Classic examples: teachers, social workers, police officers, welfare officers, immigration officials, doctors in public hospitals.

SLBs (Lipsky)

  • High discretion in applying rules
  • Effectively make policy through day-to-day decisions
  • Subject to both organizational rules and individual judgment

  • Top, middle, or line management level

Pay-for-Performance — A Double-Edged Sword

NPM's answer to motivating SLBs: look to the business sector — monetary incentives and high organizational control. Not marginal: 2/3 of civil servants in the developed world are covered by some form of pay-for-performance (OECD).

Arguments For

  • Reduces goal ambiguity — clarity on what matters
  • Strengthens employee motivation (under conditions)
  • Strengthens organizational performance (under conditions)
  • Links pay to results rather than seniority alone

Arguments Against

  • Everything not measured becomes invisible — perverse incentives
  • Creaming: select easy cases to hit metrics
  • Parking: neglect hard cases that hurt scores
  • Crowds out Public Service Motivation (PSM)

The Korean Ministry Case — Non-Monetary Rewards

Exercise in class: given potential negative behavioral consequences of performance-based pay among Korean public servants, what other ways exist to reward excellent performance?

Promotion and job transfers: Advancement within the organization as recognition — particularly valued in hierarchical cultures like Korea where seniority and rank carry significant prestige.
Training & education: Investing in an SLB's development as a signal of value and future potential — particularly attractive for those motivated by competence and growth.
Public reputation and recognition: "Public manager of the year," "public talent of the year," lifetime achievement awards. Social recognition leverages PSM — doing good publicly reinforces intrinsic motivation.
Beneficiary contact (Lesson 4): The telephone fundraiser experiment — group that met scholarship recipients raised significantly more ($$$) than the group that had no such contact ($). Direct connection to impact is a powerful, free motivational tool.

Public Service Motivation (PSM)

Definition
An individual's orientation to delivering services to people with a purpose to do good for others and society.
Self-sacrifice (avg. 3.39): Personal sacrifice to do good for others. Willingness to forgo private gain for the common good.
Attraction to public participation (avg. 4.25 — highest): PSOs as a suitable and meaningful stage for public service — distinct from volunteer work in its institutional setting.
Commitment to public values (avg. 4.07): Acting upon incorporated values like social responsibility, democracy, and fairness — values that are internalised, not just endorsed.
Compassion (avg. 3.56): Genuine concern for people, groups, or objects one wants to serve — the emotional foundation of public service work.

Four Lessons for Managing PSM

Lesson 1 — Select for PSM: Strong correlation with performance and goal fulfilment, yet hiring processes focus mainly on skills, abilities, and knowledge. Select-in: score CVs on PSM indicators (prior voluntary work, critical incidents). Select-out: salary as primary motivation is a red flag.
Lesson 2 — Socialize newcomers: On-boarding and orientation initiatives that connect new SLBs to the organization's public service mission. Encourage mentoring systems. Leaders should embody and model public service values — not just manage by metrics.
Lesson 3 — Create supportive work environments: Nurture constructive communication and performance feedback. Strengthen perceptions of autonomy and competence. Purge PSM-crowding practices: pay-for-performance, excessive job stressors, daily strain.
Lesson 4 — Connect to beneficiaries: Identify who benefits from specific jobs, programs, and organizational missions. Create opportunities for direct contact. Provide clear channels for beneficiary feedback. The scholarship fundraiser study: meeting beneficiaries increased donations dramatically.

Policy Alienation (Tummers et al., 2009)

Policy alienation is a key threat to PSM: it occurs when SLBs feel estranged from the policy they are asked to implement — either because of how it was made (powerlessness) or what it requires (meaninglessness).

Powerlessness — Process dimension

  • No control over implementation (how?)
  • Translation from policy to targets imposed top-down
  • Resource/personnel allocation decisions excluded from
  • Performance management systems aimed at control, not learning
  • Remedy: participation through workgroups, surveys, consultation rounds — giving voice AND being heard

Meaninglessness — Content dimension

  • Policy makes no sense (what & so what?)
  • The organization's contribution seems senseless
  • My individual contribution seems irrelevant
  • Three levels: policy, organization, individual
  • Remedy: active sense-making by management — engage in clarifying the purpose
Dutch disability welfare reform case (2004)
Large increase in welfare recipients (10% of total workforce) → stricter reassessment rules → insurance doctors and labour experts asked to implement. Powerlessness: doctors not consulted in policy formulation phase; little voice or discretion within the agency (big organization, major lay-offs, hierarchical culture, pressure to award jobs). Meaninglessness: official objective was "healthy to be active" but the actual operation was "money needs to be saved" — doctors felt they were making people work when they were genuinely disabled. Both dimensions combined → severe policy alienation → poor implementation quality.

Practice & Exam Questions

Knowledge · 5 pts
What are the four dimensions of Public Service Motivation (PSM)? What does research suggest about the relationship between PSM and organizational performance?
The four PSM dimensions: (1) Self-sacrifice — personal sacrifice to do good for others (avg. 3.39); (2) Attraction to public participation — PSOs as a stage for public service, highest avg. at 4.25; (3) Commitment to public values — acting on values like social responsibility, democracy, fairness (avg. 4.07); (4) Compassion — concern for people and groups one serves (avg. 3.56). Research shows a strong positive correlation between PSM and both employee performance and organizational goal fulfilment — but hiring processes mainly focus on skills and knowledge rather than PSM, missing this predictor. PSM can also be crowded out by extrinsic incentives like pay-for-performance.
Knowledge · 5 pts
Explain the two dimensions of policy alienation (powerlessness and meaninglessness). Using one case from the lecture, illustrate how policy alienation can develop in practice.
Powerlessness (process dimension): SLBs lack control over how policy is implemented — no voice in target-setting, resource allocation, or performance management system design. Meaninglessness (content dimension): the policy itself seems senseless (policy makes no sense), or the organization's contribution seems pointless, or the individual's contribution feels irrelevant. Dutch disability reform case: insurance doctors were not consulted during policy formulation (powerlessness), then asked to implement stricter reassessment rules they found harmful and contradictory to their professional ethics — reassessing people's disabilities primarily to save money rather than assess health (meaninglessness). Result: policy alienation threatened both PSM and quality of implementation.
Essay · 10 pts
Critically evaluate pay-for-performance as a motivation tool for street-level bureaucrats. Drawing on the meta-analysis (George & Van der Wal, 2023) and the concept of Public Service Motivation, under what conditions might pay-for-performance be counterproductive?
Arguments for P4P: reduces goal ambiguity, strengthens motivation and performance under certain conditions. Evidence (George & Van der Wal): impact is small at best; works better in federal government, Asian countries, and for employee-level outcomes; not a silver bullet. Counterproductive conditions: (1) When PSM is high — P4P shifts focus from intrinsic motivation (doing good) to extrinsic reward, crowding out PSM. Research shows P4P can be an antidote for PSM. (2) When performance is multi-dimensional and hard to measure — "hitting the target but missing the point"; everything not measured disappears. (3) When creaming and parking emerge — SLBs select easy cases (creaming) and neglect harder ones (parking) to hit metrics. (4) When organizational culture is already mission-driven — introducing monetary signals sends a contradictory message. Conclusion: P4P requires careful contextual calibration; for SLBs with high PSM in complex service settings, alternative motivators (beneficiary contact, autonomy, public recognition) are often more effective.

Managing Citizens

Administrative burden, co-production, and the evolving role of citizens as customers and co-producers.

What is Administrative Burden?

Definition
Costs and barriers citizens experience in their interaction with the state — can be accidental or intentional (e.g., abortion policy in the US where 1/3 of restrictions create burden by design).

Learning Costs

  • Energy to find information
  • How to navigate service delivery
  • Establishing eligibility
  • Learning the application process

Compliance Costs

  • Energy to comply with rules
  • Forms, waiting times, required documentation
  • Data already provided to other programs
  • Difficulty finding help

Psychological Costs

  • Mental discomfort with government
  • Stress, fear, stigma
  • Spill-over from learning & compliance costs
  • Affects take-up rates

Reducing Administrative Burden — What Works?

The public sector's approach to reducing burden operates at two levels: behavioral nudges (change how rules are applied) vs. structural reform (change the rules themselves).

Reducing Learning Costs

  • Simplify: centralize fragmented information into one place
  • Support: service finders, eligibility calculators (e.g., Services Australia; Flemish renovation subsidy portal)
  • Reach out: proactive contact with eligible beneficiaries rather than waiting for them to apply
  • Key actions: simplify, support, and reach out

Reducing Compliance & Psychological Costs

  • Pre-fill forms with data already held by government
  • Reduce number of forms (US tax code: 833 codes!)
  • Change tone of voice in communications → faster payments, higher compliance
  • Default options ("nudging") — opt-out rather than opt-in
  • Respectful, non-stigmatizing interaction design
Limit of nudging
Behavioral PA (nudging, default options, tone of voice) can mitigate burden at the margins but foregoes structural inequality. It addresses symptoms, not causes. Supporting third-party organizations (NPOs, volunteer services like Debt Aid Buddy) that assist vulnerable citizens is a more structural complement — but creates its own accountability questions.

The Debt Aid Buddy — Missing Link Between Policy and Practice

Second-largest debt counselling volunteer organization in the Netherlands. 2,800+ active volunteers/year. Goal: guide people to financial independence by navigating them through administrative burdens. Context: 7.6% of Dutch households have problematic debts.

Streamlining implementation: Collaboration with local governments to set up a "money counter" — grouping all financial services into one counter to reduce compliance fragmentation.
Challenging implementation: Volunteers identify where policy creates unnecessary burden and flag it through both formal channels (reporting/committees) and informal ones (direct calls to civil servants).
Influencing policy: Short feedback loops allow the organization to participate in shaping policy — a form of co-production that goes beyond service delivery into governance. Fine line between "helping" and "taking over" from the government.

Citizens as Customers vs. Co-producers

Citizens became more critical as political actors over time due to higher education levels and digitalization. Which model applies depends entirely on the reform paradigm in use.

NPM — Citizens as Customers

  • Individual choice based on purchasing power
  • Demand-driven services (e.g., cash-for-care)
  • Tripadvisor for elderly care: quality feedback, but 94% occupancy rate limits real choice
  • "Bad choice, your fault?" — structural disadvantages ignored
  • Information asymmetry: vaguer quality standards than restaurants

NPG — Citizens as Co-producers

  • Co-creation = "active involvement of end-users in various stages of the production process" (Voorberg et al., 2015)
  • Not new — but more formally institutionalized under NPG
  • Spectrum: advisory council → deciding citizen panel → advisory referendum → deciding referendum
  • Who decides is itself a political choice
Individual vs. organized citizens
An important distinction for co-production: individual citizens and organized citizens (civic organizations, associations, social movements) respond differently. Organized citizens have more capacity to participate but may not represent the most vulnerable. Individual citizens may be the intended beneficiaries but often lack the time and resources for sustained participation.

Co-production and Administrative Traditions (Voorberg et al., 2017)

Research question: do different state and governance traditions lead to different approaches to and impacts of co-production?

Multi-level government as a barrier: More governmental layers → more coordination problems → harder to implement co-production consistently. Fragmentation undermines the network coherence co-production requires.
Government support as catalyst: When government is supportive and co-production is institutionally embedded, impact is significantly higher. The institutional context shapes outcomes more than the co-production design itself.
Logical next step? Not always. Co-production is not a universal solution — its effectiveness is highly contingent on administrative tradition, governance structure, and available civil society capacity.

The Dark Side of Co-production

Deliberate rejection of responsibility: "Civic action" as cover for government retreat — the "doe-democratie" (DIY democracy) allows public services to be minimized under the guise of empowering citizens.
Failing accountability: Clear lack of responsibilities — when citizens co-produce, who is responsible when things go wrong? Democratic accountability becomes blurred.
Rising transaction costs: Many meetings, adverse reactions, delays, non-show ups — co-production can be more expensive than direct government delivery.
Loss of critical voice: Involving critical citizens in a participatory trajectory can make them less critical — co-optation risk.
Reinforced inequalities: Who participates? Consistently those who are already more educated, resourced, and organized. Vulnerable groups are systematically underrepresented.
Co-destruction of public value: Through missed opportunities, role misuse, and competing agendas, co-production can destroy rather than create public value.

Practice & Exam Questions

Knowledge · 5 pts
Explain the three types of administrative burden (learning costs, compliance costs, psychological costs). How do these differ from red tape, and why does this distinction matter for equity?
Learning costs: the energy citizens must expend to find out about a program and understand how to navigate it. Compliance costs: the effort to actually comply — filling forms, meeting waiting times, providing documentation, attending required appointments. Psychological costs: the stress, stigma, and mental discomfort arising from the first two. Difference from red tape: red tape is primarily perceived from an organizational/employee perspective; administrative burden is the citizen-side experience of the same dysfunctional rules. Equity matters because burden is not neutral — burden perceptions vary across populations. Citizens with less education, less time, or less trust in government face higher effective barriers even when formal rules are identical. This means administrative burden can systematically exclude the most vulnerable citizens from services designed to help them.
Knowledge · 5 pts
How do NPM and NPG understand the role of the citizen differently? What are the key limitations of the "customer" model of citizenship?
NPM: citizen as customer — individual choice based on purchasing power, demand-driven services (e.g., cash-for-care), competition between service providers. NPG: citizen as co-producer — active involvement in various stages of the production process (co-design, co-implement, co-evaluate). Limitations of the customer model: (1) Information asymmetry — citizens often cannot assess quality in advance (e.g., elderly care), undermining genuine choice. (2) Choice is constrained in practice — with 94% occupancy rates in elderly homes, "choice" is often theoretical. (3) "Bad choice, your fault?" — attributing poor outcomes to individual consumer decisions ignores structural inequalities. (4) Citizens are not just consumers of individual services but also collective rights-holders whose choices affect others — the customer frame misses the collective dimension of citizenship.
Essay · 10 pts
Using Voorberg et al. (2017), assess the impact of co-production on public service delivery. Under what conditions is co-production effective, and what are the risks associated with over-reliance on citizen participation?
Voorberg et al. find that co-production impact varies significantly by state and governance tradition: when government is supportive, co-production can be a strong catalyst for improved delivery; multi-level governance structures act as barriers, fragmenting coordination. Effective conditions: government support and clear institutional backing; well-defined roles for citizens and government; capacity support for citizens who cannot self-organize (e.g., Debt Aid Buddy model). Risks of over-reliance: (1) Deliberate rejection of government responsibility — using "civic action" as cover for cutting public services. (2) Equity gap — organized citizens (educated, well-resourced) are more able to co-produce, potentially reinforcing inequality. (3) Administrative tradition lock-in — co-production that works in Nordic contexts may not transfer to more hierarchical traditions. (4) Accountability deficit — when citizens produce services, democratic accountability becomes blurred. The conclusion is that co-production is a promising complement to, not replacement for, state delivery.
